About the Study

This comprehensive research explores how young individuals in Tirana, Albania engage with technology and creative communities. The study examines learning experiences, social connections, and participation patterns in spaces that increasingly shape professional development, identity, and innovation.

Methodology & Quality Assurance

The survey was carefully co-designed with community leaders from various tech and creative communities in Tirana to ensure relevance and accessibility. To maintain data quality, we implemented attention check questions (such as "If you are really reading this, select option X") throughout the survey. Only 4 responses failed these validation checks and were excluded from analysis, ensuring the reliability of our findings.

Key Findings

  • 84% of respondents cited "gaining new knowledge" as their primary motivation for attending events
  • 75% strongly prefer in-person events over online formats (43%)
  • Instagram (72%) and word-of-mouth (65.5%) are the primary channels for event discovery
  • Only 18% are frequent event attendees (monthly or more)
  • Main challenges: engagement quality, overall organization, and content depth
  • 66% of participants feel curious when attending events

Recommendations & Main Results

üìä Participation Patterns

Low Frequency of Engagement:

Most people participate rarely in events (38.7%), clearly signifying that more work needs to be done from the communities to allow for retention and more frequent participation. Furthermore, if we consider frequent attendees that participate in events once a month and/or more than once a month, only 18% of the respondents can be classified as such.

üì¢ Event Discovery Channels

Social Media & Word of Mouth Dominance:

Most events are discovered through social media, more specifically Instagram coming in first place (72%). However, surprisingly "Word of Mouth" comes in second place before any other social media (65.5%). The conclusion that can be derived is that community managers and specifically the team responsible for communication and marketing can have an initial idea on how to distribute the budget for social media promotion. However, it must not be forgotten that word of mouth is still very important and impressions and experiences drive further participation.

üí≠ Participant Experience

Positive Feelings & Room for Improvement:

Most respondents have positive feelings when participating in events such as curiosity (66%) and in terms of welcomeness level, the majority feel sufficiently welcomed (44%). However, more work can be done to create a more hospitable environment.

üè¢ Format Preferences

Strong Preference for In-Person Events:

The data clearly show a stronger preference for in-person events, though online ones have some traction as well. Even for tech communities that could be thought to be in the forefront of the usage of software tools for community engagement, the preference for in-person events is by far stronger than that for online ones, where 75% chose 3, the highest scale of preference for in-person events compared to 43% choosing the same preference level for online events.

üéØ Primary Motivations

Knowledge-Driven Participation:

In terms of the reasons people participate in the events organized by the communities, 84% of the respondents chose as the most important motivation "gaining new knowledge". The second most voted reason was "deepening knowledge". This highlights the fact that the participants are very knowledge-driven and the organizers of communities should pay careful attention to the substance of the events as opposed to more secondary factors.

‚ö†Ô∏è Main Challenges Identified

Through two open-ended questions, we identified the main challenges people encounter when they participate in community-driven events:

Challenge 1: Lack of Engagement (28.8%)

The top challenge was one that the researchers labeled as "engagement" or lack thereof. In essence, many people felt that the events were not engaging enough, for example some stating they were only superficial presentations and not allowing space for discussion.

Challenge 2: Overall Organization (24%)

The second top challenge referred to "overall organization", such as but not limited to: timing, disorganized logistics, technical issues, etc.

Challenge 3: Content Issues (20%)

The third challenge refers to issues related to the "content", such as but not limited to: the superficiality of the content, a too theoretical approach, getting out of topic, etc.

Other Challenges

The other challenges referred to "authenticity and value doubt" where the respondents doubted the value of the events and thought they were fictitious; inner-outer group dynamics between the organizers or frequent attendees and the rest of the audience; and scheduling issues.

üí° Suggestions for Improvement

What can be done better to motivate young people in Tirana to participate in more events:

Top Suggestion: New Knowledge & Content (18%)

The top suggestion was to introduce "new knowledge and content" in order to boost the motivation to participate. This is a reinforcement of the fact that the participants are knowledge-driven, a conclusion that emerged throughout the study.

Second Priority: More Engagement (15.6%)

Make the events more engaging by allowing for more space for discussions and having the organizers serve as facilitators for wider engagement during the event and not only with the people they are already familiar with. This would require the organizers to truly inhabit their role and become genuine community builders.

Third Priority: Networking Opportunities (14.2%)

Create structured opportunities for participants to connect with each other, not just with speakers or organizers.

Additional Suggestions

Other suggestions encompassed: introducing "transactional values" in events such as food, drinks, or other tangible benefits; a "clear agenda" of what the events will comprise; and matters of content such as depth and practicality to ensure true learning.
